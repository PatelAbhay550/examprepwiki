# Fundamental Rights

**Fundamental Rights** are the basic human rights enshrined in Part III (Articles 12-35) of the Indian Constitution. These rights are justiciable, meaning they can be enforced through courts of law.

## Overview

The Fundamental Rights were borrowed from the Constitution of the United States and were incorporated to ensure individual dignity and create conditions for full development of personality. Originally, there were seven fundamental rights, but after the 44th Amendment (1978), the Right to Property was removed from the list, leaving six fundamental rights.

## Classification of Fundamental Rights

### 1. Right to Equality (Articles 14-18)

**Article 14: Equality before law**
- The State shall not deny any person equality before the law or equal protection of laws within Indian territory
- This is the foundation of all other rights

**Article 15: Prohibition of discrimination**
- Prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth
- Exception: State can make special provisions for women, children, and socially disadvantaged groups

**Article 16: Equality of opportunity in public employment**
- Equal opportunity for all citizens in matters of public employment
- State can make reservations for backward classes, SCs, and STs

**Article 17: Abolition of Untouchability**
- Untouchability is abolished and its practice is forbidden
- Enforcement through Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955

**Article 18: Abolition of Titles**
- No titles except military and academic distinctions
- Citizens cannot accept titles from foreign states

### 2. Right to Freedom (Articles 19-22)

**Article 19: Six Freedoms** (available only to citizens)
1. Freedom of speech and expression
2. Freedom to assemble peacefully without arms
3. Freedom to form associations or unions
4. Freedom to move freely throughout India
5. Freedom to reside and settle in any part of India
6. Freedom to practice any profession, occupation, trade, or business

**Reasonable Restrictions:** State can impose reasonable restrictions on these freedoms in the interest of:
- Sovereignty and integrity of India
- Security of State
- Friendly relations with foreign states
- Public order, decency, or morality
- Contempt of court, defamation, or incitement to offense

**Article 20: Protection in respect of conviction for offenses**
- No ex-post-facto law
- No double jeopardy
- No self-incrimination

**Article 21: Protection of life and personal liberty**
- Most important fundamental right
- Expanded through judicial interpretation to include:
  - Right to privacy
  - Right to education
  - Right to livelihood
  - Right to clean environment
  - Right to health
  - Right to shelter

**Article 21A: Right to Education**
- Added by 86th Amendment (2002)
- Free and compulsory education for children aged 6-14 years
- Implemented through RTE Act, 2009

**Article 22: Protection against arrest and detention**
- Right to be informed of grounds of arrest
- Right to consult and be defended by a legal practitioner
- Right to be produced before magistrate within 24 hours

### 3. Right against Exploitation (Articles 23-24)

**Article 23: Prohibition of traffic in human beings and forced labor**
- Begar (forced labor) is abolished
- Exception: State can impose compulsory service for public purposes

**Article 24: Prohibition of child labor**
- No child below 14 years can be employed in factories, mines, or hazardous employment

### 4. Right to Freedom of Religion (Articles 25-28)

**Article 25: Freedom of conscience and free profession, practice, and propagation of religion**
- Subject to public order, morality, and health
- State can regulate secular activities associated with religious practices

**Article 26: Freedom to manage religious affairs**
- Every religious denomination has the right to:
  - Establish and maintain institutions for religious purposes
  - Manage its own affairs
  - Own and acquire property
  - Administer such property

**Article 27: Freedom from taxation for promotion of any religion**

**Article 28: Freedom from religious instruction in state-funded institutions**

### 5. Cultural and Educational Rights (Articles 29-30)

**Article 29: Protection of interests of minorities**
- Right to conserve distinct language, script, or culture
- Protection from discrimination in state-aided institutions

**Article 30: Right of minorities to establish and administer educational institutions**
- Religious and linguistic minorities can establish educational institutions
- State cannot discriminate in granting aid

### 6. Right to Constitutional Remedies (Article 32)

**Article 32: Remedies for enforcement of Fundamental Rights**
- Called the "heart and soul" of the Constitution by Dr. B.R. Ambedkar
- Right to move Supreme Court for enforcement of Fundamental Rights
- Supreme Court can issue five types of writs:
  1. **Habeas Corpus** - To produce a person before court
  2. **Mandamus** - Command to perform public duty
  3. **Prohibition** - To prevent lower courts from exceeding jurisdiction
  4. **Certiorari** - To quash order of lower court
  5. **Quo Warranto** - To inquire into legality of claim to public office

## Significance and Importance

1. **Protection of Individual Liberty**: Guards against arbitrary state action
2. **Foundation of Democracy**: Essential for democratic functioning
3. **Social Equality**: Promotes social justice and equality
4. **Dignity of Individual**: Ensures human dignity and personality development
5. **Limitation on State Power**: Acts as check on legislative and executive actions

## Important Supreme Court Cases

### Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)
- Established the **Basic Structure Doctrine**
- Fundamental Rights are part of basic structure
- Parliament cannot amend Constitution to destroy its basic structure

### Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)
- Expanded scope of Article 21
- Procedure must be just, fair, and reasonable
- Interconnection between Articles 14, 19, and 21

### Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan (1997)
- Laid down guidelines for prevention of sexual harassment at workplace
- Demonstrated court's power to fill legislative vacuum

## Restrictions and Limitations

### During Normal Times
- Reasonable restrictions under Article 19
- State can impose limitations for public interest
- Must pass test of reasonableness

### During Emergency
- Articles 19, 20, 21, and 22 can be suspended (Article 358)
- Other rights continue but remedies suspended (Article 359)
- Right to life under Article 21 cannot be suspended even during emergency

## Enforcement Mechanisms

1. **Judicial Review**: Courts can strike down laws violating Fundamental Rights
2. **Writ Jurisdiction**: Article 32 (Supreme Court) and Article 226 (High Courts)
3. **Public Interest Litigation (PIL)**: Relaxed locus standi for enforcement
4. **National Human Rights Commission**: Statutory body for protection of rights

## Difference from Directive Principles

| Fundamental Rights | Directive Principles |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Justiciable | Non-justiciable |
| Negative obligations on State | Positive obligations on State |
| Political democracy | Social and economic democracy |
| Can be enforced through courts | Cannot be enforced through courts |

## Recent Developments

### Right to Privacy (2017)
- Declared fundamental right under Article 21
- Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India
- Led to scrutiny of Aadhaar scheme

### Section 377 Verdict (2018)
- Decriminalized consensual homosexual acts
- Expanded scope of Article 21 - right to dignity

### Triple Talaq (2019)
- Declared unconstitutional
- Protection of Muslim women's rights under Articles 14, 15, and 21

## Conclusion

Fundamental Rights are the cornerstone of Indian democracy, ensuring individual liberty while balancing societal interests. Their dynamic interpretation by judiciary has kept them relevant to changing times. Understanding these rights is crucial for UPSC Civil Services Examination, especially for General Studies Paper II (Polity).
